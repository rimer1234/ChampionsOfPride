Marsha P. Johnson

1945 - 1992

Early Life
Marsha P. Johnson was born on August 24, 1945 in Elizabeth, New Jersey.
She was the fifth of seven children in her family.
Johnson experienced discrimination and bullying for being effeminate as a child.
She moved to New York City in 1963 and began working as a prostitute to support herself.
Achievements
Johnson became involved in the LGBTQ+ rights movement in the 1960s, and was present during the Stonewall uprising in 1969.
She co-founded the Street Transvestite Action Revolutionaries (STAR) with Sylvia Rivera in 1970, an organization that provided support for homeless and runaway LGBTQ+ youth.
Johnson was a drag queen and performer, often performing at the popular gay club, The Stonewall Inn.
Johnson was also an activist for AIDS awareness and treatment in the 1980s.
Challenges
In the 1970s, Johnson experienced a series of mental health breakdowns and spent time in and out of psychiatric hospitals.
She also continued to engage in sex work, not knowing any other way to make money, and continued to get arrested.
In 1990, Johnson was diagnosed with H.I.V. She spoke publicly about her diagnosis and how people should not be afraid of those with the disease in a June 26, 1992 interview.
She died on July 6, 1992, under mysterious circumstances, and her death was initially ruled a suicide, but many believed she was murdered.
Personal Life
She was known for her unique and colorful style, which included wearing flower crowns and carrying a purse filled with flowers and other trinkets.
According to her nephew, Johnson always maintained a close but fraught relationship with her family back in New Jersey.
In an interview Johnson did for a 1972 book, she said her ambition was “to see gay people liberated and free and to have equal rights that other people have in America.”
